Ana María Montoya

Ph.D. candidate

Duke University

I am a Ph.D. candidate in the Department of Political Science at Duke University and an USIP-Minerva Peace and Security Scholar.

My research interests lie at the intersection of comparative political institutions and political behavior. As a comparativist, my research agenda seeks to understand the factors that lead to the emergence of the rule of law in post-conflict settings. A more complete understanding of the emergence and maintenance of the rule of law requires an approach that incorporates institutional factors as well as the micro- foundations of behavior at both the elite and mass levels.

My dissertation investigates how land courts enforced their rulings in Colombia’s ongoing land restitution program, and, in turn, why politicians and bureaucrats comply with these judicial decisions protecting property rights, while others do not, both at the national and local level.

At Duke, I have taught interdisciplinary courses at the Sanford School of Public Policy. I am affiliated with the PhD Lab in Digital Knowledge where we engage the challenges and promises of digital research and pedagogy.

Before coming to Duke, I worked at the Latin American Public Opinion Project (LAPOP) at Vanderbilt University. Currently, I am affiliated with the DevLab@Duke where we collaborate on the design and evaluation of international development programs. I conducted an evaluation of the USAID/Colombia’s Land and Rural Development Program (LRDP, 2013-2018).

Working Papers

The Judicial Battle over Land in Transitional Contexts (Under review)
Ana María Montoya

Why do rural poor fight to reassert their land property rights? In developing countries, property rights over land are the most fragile and weakest defined rights. In this paper, I address the question of why citizens bring land claims to courts by studying the effect of the introduction of the land restitution program on the peasants’ willingness to reassert their land rights in poor rural Colombia. I argue that land tenure security should come from the formal recognition of a right, but also should be enforced and secured by the state. Through an original dataset collected while I conducted a performance evaluation of a USAID’s Land and Rural Development Program (LRDP), I analyze a household survey (N=1462) that was conducted in 50 municipalities across different regions in the countryside. First, using this quasi-experimental design, I estimate the impact of the strength of prior property rights, their attitudes towards the state and judicial system and the expectation of enforcement on the decision to bring a land claim before the Land Restitution Unit. Second, using a conjoint experiment, I estimate the causal effect of these components into the decision-making of bringing a case. I find strong support for the argument that both the strength of prior property rights before the dispossession and the individuals’ expectation of enforcement matter when deciding to bring a case to courts.

Capacity or a Will Problem? The Compliance with the Land Restitution Orders at the Subnational Level in Colombia (2011-2021)
Ana María Montoya

How is the rule of law enforced “on the ground”? In particular, why do public authorities comply with judicial orders protecting land property rights, while others do not? Although court involvement has become more common, judgments are not always a guarantee that the right will be protected. Government officials, often tasked with implementing measures to guarantee the protection of the rights, are not always in compliance. My argument aims to identify whether politicians have the capacity to comply with and enforce rulings, and once the capacity conditions are met, if they are willing to comply with the ruling or not. In this paper, I use a regression discontinuity design (RDD) to estimate the causal effect of partisanship (proxy for political will) on enforcement in close mayoral elections in 2011, 2015, 2019. This empirical strategy allows me to exploit the fact that a mayor’s partisan affiliation changes discontinuously at the threshold between a party’s victory or loss. The observable implication is that municipalities where a candidate in favor of the restitution program barely won should be, on average, indistinguishable from municipalities in which a candidate against the restitution program barely lost. Since preferences towards the restitution program align with the ideological dimension between left-wing and right-wing political parties, I expect that left-wing party would be more favorable towards the implementation of the land restitution program than right-wing parties. On the contrary, there are ample evidence on how right-wing parties have represented the interests of landed elites, and how they even have formed alliances with paramilitaries, one of the main responsible actors of the forced displacement during the conflict. Results provide evidence that a narrow left-wing victory leads to an additional four productive projects implemented, a sizable effect equivalent to almost two standard deviation, and over 1,36 times the same mean. Moreover, the effect of a left-wing electoral victory on the implementation of land restitution outcomes remains positive and significant for different bandwidth and robustness checks.

Strategic Deference: How Judges Use Language to Enhance Compliance.
Ana María Montoya

Implementation of judicial decisions is itself an enforcement problem. As a focal point in this problem, compliance is far from being a likely outcome. Both politicians and bureaucrats routinely delay the implementation of judicial policies. Some of these policies are ignored completely. Yet judges also create the conditions for delay and noncompliance by writing vague judicial opinions. When and how courts achieve compliance, and what impact the clarity of their opinions have on compliance, remain largely untested. In this paper, I develop an argument that analyzes a situation where vagueness, conceptualized as broad orders, allows judges to deal with limited policy expertise while they delegate power to public authorities with greater expertise to achieve the ends desired by judges. Unlike other strategic accounts of the use of vagueness to hide defiance from policymakers, I argue that since judges use vagueness not only to allow agents to adapt policy in light of their expertise, but also as a strategy to enhance voluntary compliance by recognizing their expertise. More importantly, judges use broad orders as a way to not impose detailed orders on how things should be executed. By doing this, judges avoid adverse reactions and potential delays from policymakers. Using an original dataset of 90,230 orders contained in the judicial decisions made by Colombian Land Restitution Courts and linguistic measures derived from natural language processing techniques, I exploit exogenous variation from random assignment of cases to judges with different propensities to write precise judicial orders. Empirical results strongly support these theoretical expectations.
